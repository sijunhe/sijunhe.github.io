# 1. High-Tech Marketing Illusion

![Tech_Adoption_Cycle](https://s3-us-west-1.amazonaws.com/sijunhe-blog/plots/post13/tech_adoption_cycle.png)

### Technology Adoption Life Cycle

- The **Technology Adoption Life Cycle** is a model for understanding the acceptance of new products. 
- Each group represents a unique psychographical profile toward a discontinuous innovation based on a new technology
	- **Innovators**: Pursue new technology produces aggresively. Intrigued with any fundamental advance and often make a technology purchase simply for the pleasure of exploring the new device's properties
	- **early adopter**: Buy into new product concepts very early in their life cycle. When there is a strong match, they are willing to base their buying decisions upon it. They do not rely on well-established references in marking these buying decisions.
	- **early majority**: Driven by a strong sense of practicality. They want well-established references before investing substantiall. Roughly *a third* of the whole adoption life cycle and winning their business is key to profit and growth.
	- **late majority**: They wait until something has become an established standard, even then they want to see lots of support and tend to buy from large, well-stablished companies. Roughly *a third* of the total buying population. Courting its favor is highly profitable.
	- **laggard**: Simply don't want anything to do with new technology. The only tie they ever buy a technological product is when it is buried very deep inside another product. Generally regarded as not worth pursuing.
- The way to develop a high-tech market is to work the curve left to right and use each "captured" group as a reference base for going on to market to the next group.

### Cracks in the Bell Curve
- Between any two psychographic groups are gaps, which symbolizes the dissociation between the two groups, that is, the difficulty any group will have in accepting a new product if it is presented the same way as it was to the group to its immediate left.
- **First Crack** between innovators and early adopters occurs when a hot technology product cannot be readily translated into a major new benefit. The enthuiast loves it for its architecture, but nobody else can figure out how to start using it. Example the book gave was **Neural Networks**! Apparently when this chapter was revised (1998), Neural Networks has not shown any commerical success and won't be until the 2010s. **I'd say in 2017, Deep Learning is in the Early Majority cycle now.**
- **Second Crack** falls between the early majority and the late majority. By this point the technology product is well-developed and has been absorbed into the mainstream. The key issue has to do with demands on the end user to be technologically competent. When a product reaches this point, it must be made increasingly easier to adopt in order to continue being successful.

### The Chasm
- The deep and dividing *chasm* that separates the early adopters from the early majority is the most formidable and unforgiving transition. 
- The early adopter is buying a tech product as *change agent* and expect to get a jump on the competition. The expect a radical discontinuity and are prepared to bear with the inevitable bugs and glitches. 
- The early majority want a *productivity improvement*, a evolution instead of a revolution. They want it to work properly.
- Early adopters do not make good references for the early majority, for the above reasons.
